## Abstract 

We analyse SGD training of a shallow, fully connected network in the NTK scaling and provide a quantitative theory of the catapult phase. We identify an explicit criterion separating two behaviours: When an explicit function 

G, depending only on the kernel, learning rate η and data, is positive, SGD produces large NTK-flattening spikes with high probability; when 

G < 0, their probability decays like (n/η )−ϑ/ 2, for an explicitly characterised 

ϑ ∈ (0 , ∞). This yields a concrete parameter-dependent explanation for why such spikes may still be observed at practical widths.

1.1 Contribution 

The contribution of this work is a rigorous understanding of the structure of the catapult phase in SGD and its contribution to finding flatter minima. We use the neural tangent kernel (NTK), which in our examples reduces to a scalar λ or pair of scalars, as a measure of the sharpness. We introduce and rigorously analyse a model example, following [ 52 ], for shallow, fully connected networks in the NTK scaling, in the sharp minimum range ηλ ∼ 1.In this setting, we are able to completely categorise when spikes are guaranteed, and how likely they are when not guaranteed. Even for such a simplified model, the catapult phase shows rich and nontrivial internal structure which is not present in the case of full-batch gradient descent. The structure is illustrated in Figure 1, stated precisely in Theorem 1 and exemplified in Section 1.3. We summarise as follows. Defining a large spike to be an event where the loss 

ℓ(Θ( t)) reaches the threshold n/η where the network escapes the linear regime of lazy training and where the curvature is reduced by O(1) , the results may be informally summarised as follows. We prove that: 1. for a certain phase of initial curvature λ0, a large spike is guaranteed (Theorems 3 and 5); 2. in a further regime, in which they are not guaranteed, they remain polynomially likely , in that their probability decays like (n/η )−ϑ/ 2, for some ϑ = ϑ(λ0) ∈ (0 , ∞)

(Theorem 4 and 6); 3. large spikes are, up to exponentially unlikely events, the only way in which the lazy training regime can be escaped and the curvature reduced (Proposition 4.2); 4. and, in the case where the activation function of the first layer in the rectified linear unit (ReLU) and a certain asymmetric initialisation is imposed, the dynamics decouple into two copies of the linear model, to which each of the previous points applies.

Here, we follow the convention [ 53 ] that a ‘catapult’ refers to a single update step in which the learning rate η exceeds a per-sample critical rate, causing an instantaneous increase in the running loss, and distinguish between this and a spike where the overall loss has become large. Both points 1-2 allow direct computation from the data. The distinction between the two regimes where spikes are typical or atypical is governed by the sign of a function 

G(λ0), which is given explicitly from the data by (1.7) . The function ϑ(λ0) in point 2 is characterised as unique positive zero of a certain convex function (1.8) , and hence may be found in practice from the data by interval bisection. The points 2-3 above arise out of the machinery of large deviations. Often, the theory of large deviations leads to estimates of probabilities decaying exponentially 

∼ e−αn , α > 0, and for the values of 10 6 ≤ n ≤ 10 12 used in practice, such events may be excluded from consideration. In contrast, the polynomial decay of probabilities discussed in (2) means that such events may retain appreciable probability in practice when the exponent 

ϑ is not too large. An example, where the probability remains on the order (n/η )−ϑ/ 2 ≈ 0.25 

for n = 10 12 , is given in Section 1.3.5. In (3), we follow the maxim of large deviations in describing the ‘least unlikely’ way in which random fluctuations via the minibatching noise can reduce the curvature, and we will see that large kernel reductions without a spike are exponentially unlikely.

Motivation of the Model: High Dimensionality and the Ubiquity of Spikes We first give a motivation of the models described by Theorem 1, as well as an informal argument that it partially explains the ubiquity of spikes in practice. In addition to the large numbers of parameters, the dimension d

of the domain of a learned function may also be in the millions or billions. In various models studied in the literature, the dynamics in the different directions completely or partially decouple and evolve independently of one another over the training process, for example in 

9Spikes in SGD: an LDP View Gess, Heydecker 

SGD-related methods in matrix factori-sation [ 19 ], see also [ 32 , Proposition 5] for a similar observation. The same also applies to Mixture-of-Expert type architectures [10 , 40 , 38 , 42 , 44 , 17 ] if the routing coefficients are kept constant after initialisation, as is done in practice in [18, 46]. The models in Theorem 1 are chosen as a caricature of the per-channel dynamics of such a model. Globally, we imagine that choosing any datapoint xi from the whole training dataset only affects the parameters of one channel 1 ≤ α(i) ≤ d, producing a loss through a subnetwork of one of the forms in Theorem 1 through a scalar si = ⟨eα, xi⟩. Even if the input data xi are normalised, si will (in general) not be, and it is possible for multiple datapoints to contribute to each channel. Theorem 1 now governs the probability of a spike in each channel α, with a decay rate no worse than (n/η )−ϑα/2 as soon as (1.6) is satisfied. We now consider the evolution of the loss of the whole network. If, for some 

θ ≥ 0, a significant fraction of the channels have ϑα ≤ θ, then the total number of spikes is approximately a Poisson random variable, with mean of the order O(d(n/η )−θ/ 2). In particular, since θ > 0 can be small, a large number of spikes can be caused without imposing any polynomial relation on d, n .

Convergence of SGD, Catapult Mechanism The basic analysis of SGD, see for example [ 33 , 24 ], shows that, for sufficiently small η, all but a vanishing fraction of the time is spent near critical points. While the landscape of ℓ may contain many saddle points [ 13 ], it is known that SGD avoids, with high probability, spurious critical regions, see for example [ 36 ] and references therein. The appearance of large spikes in the loss of SGD has been attributed empirically to the catapult mechanism [ 53 ], and as a jumping-off point, we take the models discussed in [ 27 , 52 ] to understand how the catapult mechanism interacts with SGD noise. In this setting, the results presented in Theorem 1 above allow us to rigorously validate many of the results of [ 53 ], while allowing refinements of others, see Example 1.3.5. Both the informal analysis given above and the rigorous proofs can also be extended to cover the quadratic networks studied in [ 52 ] without significantly changing the analysis, replacing (1.12) above by [52, Equations (11-12)]. 

Implicit bias, edge of stability, and sharpness. Beyond convergence, recent literature has sought to understood which implicit bias mechanisms of gradient methods help methods find minima with additional structural properties, see [ 37 , 47 ] and references therein. Blanc et al. [ 6], Li et al. [31 ], Shalova et al. [43 ] and Marion-Chizat [ 35 ] showed that, on a submanifold of minima, SGD will further select points for smaller curvature, typically on a longer time scale than the original convergence to the minima. Recent works have also identified the edge of stability effect [ 2 , 3, 11 , 51 ], where gradient descent algorithms tend to sharpen curvatures below the critical value 2/η , and reduce curvatures above this value. 

Lazy training and the NTK limit. One of the insights driving recent breakthroughs on proving the convergence of gradient descent algorithms [ 15 , 29 ] in problems such as (1.2) is the observation that, when F is a wide neural network, each component θi, 1 ≤ i ≤ n will hardly vary over the evolution in the limit n → ∞ , a phenomenon now called lazy training 

[9]. As a result, the whole network behaves like a purely linear model around its linearisation [ 22 ]. Consistently with this, the models analysed here behave, in epochs between spikes, close to purely linear models. The (im)probability of the spikes is determined by the random dynamics of the linear part, while the spikes themselves allow the system to (briefly) leave the lazy regime and show nonlinear effects. 

Diffusion and large-deviation perspectives. An important step in understanding the typical behaviour of SGD is to view it as the discretisation of a stochastic differential equation, going back to Li [28]. This insight led to a heuristic explanation for SGD finding flatter minima via large deviation theory by [ 21 ], made precise by Azizian et al. [ 4, 5 ]. The 

10 Spikes in SGD: an LDP View Gess, Heydecker 

viewpoint of the current work is complementary to the works [ 4, 5], which show a preference for minimisers of a certain quasipotential in the limit η → 0 with dimension parameter n

held fixed, while we consider the opposite sort of limit n → ∞ with η held fixed. In both cases, the transitions between the basins of attraction of different minima is driven by large deviations and produces short time intervals where the loss is large, although driven in the two cases by different mechanisms. The models of networks we use for tractable, exact analysis are similar to, and slightly more complicated than, diagonal networks, where the benefit of stochasticity was identified by Pesme [37, 16]. 

A New Criterion for Instability & The Edge of Stochastic Stability One of the key steps of the large-deviation analysis is the sharp identification of a criterion distinguishing blow up and convergence for the SGD in the linear, scalar case which appears to be new relative to the literature. We distinguish between the phenomena of almost sure linear blowup , which drives a large spike of the nonlinear dynamics, and blowup in expectation ,where the mean of the process diverges. An analysis of the latter condition [ 1] leads to a condition called the stochastic edge of stability . This distinction is not, of course, specific to SGD, and may be illustrated by whether one considers the process

How Spikes End A byproduct of the analysis, which is relevant in quantifying the reduction in the curvature before and after a spike, is to identify the ways in which spikes 

end after a large threshold has been reached. We show that three distinct effects are possible: gradual curvature reduction beyond a critical threshold; neuron deactivation in models with ReLU activation; or spike collapse, where nonlinear effects end the spike in a single step. 

Phases of Curvature vs Phases of Learning Rate The distinct phases and their phenomena can be interpreted in two different ways. In [ 27 , 52 ], the different phases are described by the learning rate η > 0, whereas we refer to them as depending on curvature 

λ. In fact, all of the quantities G(λ) = G(λ; η, {data }), ϑ(λ) = ϑ(λ; η, {data }) depend only on λ, η through the product λη , which allows an exact translation of the two viewpoints. We take the viewpoint that G, ϑ are functions of λ, which determine how the curvature λ(t)

will vary over the course of the training. The alternative viewpoint would be to describe Theorems 3 - 4 as describing, for a given initial curvature λ0, which learning rates η make curvature reductions via a spike likely, or at least not too unlikely.
