# 1 Introduction 

Liu et al. [10] introduced Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs), in which the fixed nonlinear activations of classical multilayer perceptrons are replaced by learnable univariate functions placed on edges. The design is motivated by the classical Kolmogorov–Arnold (KA) representation theorem [1, 2], which asserts that every continuous function of several variables can be written as a finite superposition of continuous functions of a single variable together with addition. In Sprecher’s refinement [3], every f ∈ C0([0 , 1] n) admits a representation of the form

The structural hypothesis under which we work is that f is obtained from a finite computation tree with N internal nodes and that each node depends on at most a constant number s of variables. The main theorem is formulated on the unit cube [0 , 1] n; Section 3 provides an explicit affine reduction from functions on a general compact set 

K ⊂ Rn to this setting. This compositional sparsity hypothesis holds for a wide class of physically meaningful functions such as symbolic expressions, classical mechanics equations, and low-rank tensor decompositions. 

Contributions. Our contributions are as follows. 1. We introduce the layer-wise Lipschitz product P (KAN) as the architecturally relevant quantity and distinguish it carefully from the ambient Lipschitz constant Lip(KAN). 2. We prove a main structural theorem (Theorem 4.3) that controls P (KAN f ) uniformly in the input dimension n for compositionally structured f .3. For the standard operation families treated in Section 5, we verify inductively that the quan-titative range bounds required in (A4) can be established explicitly, using the hypothesis that leaves are coordinate projections xp ∈ [0 , 1]. 4. We provide numerical experiments confirming the theoretical bounds and a Lean 4 sketch map-ping the induction to a formalizable statement in Mathlib. 

Related work. The representation theorem [1, 2] and its analytic sharpenings [3, 4, 5] form the classical foundation. Mazurkiewicz [6] is invoked only for an historical comment on the generic non-differentiability of continuous functions. Neural-network realizations of compositional super-position and depth-separation results appear in Poggio et al. [9], Montanelli and Yang [7], and Schmidt-Hieber [8]. The KAN architecture itself is due to Liu et al. [10], and the hierarchical back-propagation-free variant HKAN is due to Dudek [11].

2.2 Definitions for the main theorem 

We fix a finite set Op of admissible operations; each op ∈ Op is a continuous function op : Rs → R

for some arity s ≥ 1 (with s = 1 for unary operations such as sin , cos and s = 2 for binary operations such as + , −, ×). 

Definition 2.2 (Computation tree and compositional depth) . A computation tree T for a function 

f : [0 , 1] n → R is a rooted finite tree whose leaves are labelled by coordinate projections xp

(p ∈ { 1, . . . , n }) and whose internal nodes are labelled by operations op ∈ Op. Binary operations such as + , −, × are represented as binary nodes with two children; unary operations such as sin , cos are represented as unary nodes with a single child. The compositional depth d(f ) is the length of the longest root-to-leaf path; the total node count total nodes( f ) = N is the number of internal nodes of the fixed such tree T .

# 7 Discussion 

7.1 What is proved 

Theorem 4.3 shows that compositionally structured functions admit deep KAN representations with: 

• layer-wise Lipschitz product P (KAN) ≤ max( COp , 1) Lf (with Lf = P 

> v

cop( v) ≤ cmax · N ) inde-pendent of the input dimension n, addressing a gap noted in Liu et al. [10]; 

• layer width linear in n + N , neither quadratic nor exponential; 

• approximation rate matching the optimal B-spline rate of the same order, multiplied by the node count N .

7.2 Open questions 

• Compositional sparsity verification (Gap b). Determining whether a given f satisfies (A2) for a fixed s appears to be computationally hard in general (verification of compositional structure is related to circuit decomposition problems); Theorem 4.3 therefore treats (A2) as an explicit 13 hypothesis. For functions with known structure (physical equations, symbolic expressions), (A2) is checkable. 

• Width lower bound. Our construction gives nl ≤ n+2 wmax N , and the input layer satisfies n0 = n;interior layers can be narrower. Whether the upper bound can be tightened for all layers remains open. 

• Lower bound on P (KAN) . We know of no compositionally sparse function class on which Pmin →∞ with n. The upper bound P ≤ 1 in Corollary 4.4 is attained at P = 1 for all tested examples (Proposition 4.1 and Section 6.2); whether P < 1 is achievable for a different construction is open. 

• Lean 4 formalisation. The inductive structure of Theorem 4.3 maps naturally onto a CompGraph 

inductive type in Mathlib; see Appendix A. 

7.3 Connection to HKAN (Dudek 2025) 

The hierarchical KAN of Dudek [11] stacks KAN layers without backpropagation, solving each level as a convex least-squares problem. Theorem 4.3 provides a theoretical mechanism and tem-plate for avoiding Lipschitz explosion in hierarchical KAN constructions, under the compositional and primitive-block hypotheses (A1)–(A5). It does not constitute an unrestricted guarantee for arbitrary HKAN training or stacked architectures beyond those hypotheses. Under (A1)–(A5), 

P (KAN) remains bounded by max( COp , 1) Lf (with Lf ≤ cmax · N ) regardless of the depth of the stack.
