Abstract 

We investigate a ratio sequence derived from the factorization of pm−1 + 1, where pn denotes the nth prime. For each m ≥ 3, write pm−1 + 1 = LmRm with Lm the largest prime factor. Restricting to those m for which Lm > m (equivalently, m ∈ A223881), we obtain a multiset of values Rm. Sorting the distinct Rm by decreasing frequency yields a new sequence beginning 2, 3, 4, 8, 6, 12 , 10 , 14 , 15 , 18 , 20 , 24 , . . . . 

This article explains how this construction arises naturally from the structure of A223881, why the “family” phenomenon appears in plots of pm−1 + 1, and how the frequency ordering of 

Rm captures the dominant families. Additionally, we propose a heuristic asymptotic model explaining the observed frequency ordering via classical results on primes in arithmetic progressions and support the model with numerical log-log analysis. 

## 1 Background: The Origin of the Construction 

1.1 The OEIS sequence A223881 

The sequence A223881 is defined as the set of integers m ≥ 3 for which 

m!

pm−1 + 1 /∈ Z.

This condition is equivalent to LPF( pm−1 + 1) > m, 

where LPF( n) denotes the largest prime factor of n.Thus A223881 consists of those indices m for which pm−1 + 1 has a prime factor exceeding m.This condition is surprisingly restrictive. For most m, the number pm−1 + 1 has all of its prime factors ≤ m, and such m do not belong to A223881. The exceptional values of m form a sparse but structured set. 

1.2 The “family” phenomenon 

When one plots the values of pm−1 + 1 for m ∈ A223881, an unexpected pattern emerges: the points lie on several distinct curves, each resembling a smooth growth trajectory. These curves are the “families” observed in computational experiments. 1

To understand this, write 

pm−1 + 1 = LmRm,

where Lm is the largest prime factor and Rm is the complementary factor. Since Lm > m , the value Lm is forced to be large, and therefore Rm must be relatively small. Thus each fixed value of R defines a curve 

pm−1 + 1 = R · Lm,

and the points with the same R cluster along that curve. The most common values of R correspond to the most prominent families.

## 7 Prime Values of R

For R to be prime, the number pm−1 + 1 must factor as 

pm−1 + 1 = R · Lm,

with both R and Lm prime. Such factorizations are rare, and only R = 2 and R = 3 appear among the most frequent values. Larger prime values of R do occur but extremely infrequently. ## 11 Conclusion 

The frequency-ordered ratio sequence derived from the factorization of pm−1 + 1 provides a new perspective on the structure of A223881. The dominant families correspond to the smallest and most frequent values of Rm, while a long tail reflects the diversity of factorizations of pm−1 + 1. The heuristic model suggests that the ordering is governed asymptotically by inverse Euler totient weights, linking the phenomenon to classical laws of prime distribution in arithmetic pro-gressions. 4References
