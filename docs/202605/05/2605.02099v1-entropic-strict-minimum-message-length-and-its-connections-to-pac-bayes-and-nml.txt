## ABSTRACT 

We introduce entropic strict minimum message length (SMML), a risk-sensitive generalization of strict minimum message length coding. The proposed criterion replaces expected two-part codelength under the prior predictive distribution with an exponential certainty equivalent, thereby defining a one-parameter family of coding rules that interpolates between Bayesian average-case coding and worst-case minimax coding. We show that ordinary SMML is recovered in the risk-neutral limit, while the extreme risk-sensitive limit yields a minimax codelength criterion; when centered by the oracle maximum likelihood codelength, this criterion coincides with the normalized maximum likelihood (NML) minimax-regret principle. We further prove that entropic SMML admits a variational characterization as a Kullback–Leibler-regularized worst-case expected codelength, giving it a PAC–Bayes-type interpretation. We establish a joint asymptotic theory linking the sample size n and the risk parameter τ , showing that in regular parametric models the transition between Bayesian, robust, and minimax coding regimes occurs on a logarithmic scale. For regular exponential families, the fixed-codebook partition remains affine in sufficient-statistic space, while the codepoints satisfy a tilted moment-matching condition and admit an interpretation as tilted Bregman centroids. These results position entropic SMML as an information-theoretic bridge between MML, PAC–Bayes, and MDL.

## 1 Introduction 

Minimum message length (MML) [ 1, 2, 3, 4 ] and minimum description length (MDL) [ 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 , 10 , 11 ] are two of the most influential coding-based approaches to statistical inference and model selection. Both arise from the principle that learning may be viewed as compression, yet they differ in how uncertainty and optimality are formalized. In its strict two-part form, strict minimum message length (SMML) [ 2, 4 ] builds a finite codebook by minimizing the 

expected codelength under the prior predictive distribution, and is therefore inherently Bayesian and average-case in nature. By contrast, normalized maximum likelihood (NML) [ 7, 9, 11 ], the canonical code in MDL, is characterized by a minimax-regret principle and optimizes worst-case performance relative to the maximum likelihood (ML) code. Although these paradigms are often presented as reflecting different statistical philosophies, both are coding rules for assigning codelength to data. This paper introduces a new coding principle that bridges these regimes. The starting point is to replace the expected codelength in strict MML by an entropic (or risk-sensitive) certainty equivalent, yielding a one-parameter family of coding criteria indexed by a risk parameter τ > 0. For small τ , the criterion behaves like the ordinary SMML objective.

As τ increases, it places increasing emphasis on rare but costly codewords, smoothly interpolating toward a worst-case coding principle. This entropic deformation is natural from several perspectives. First, exponential certainty equivalents are canonical in risk-sensitive decision theory, statistical physics, large deviations, and the theory of entropic risk measures, where they provide a smooth interpolation between expectation and maximization [ 12 , 13 ]. Second, the new criterion admits a variational representation in terms of Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence [ 14 ], namely the Gibbs–Donsker–Varadhan variational formula [ 12 , 15 ], and therefore has a PAC–Bayes-type interpretation as a KL-regularized worst-case expected codelength over posterior tilts of the prior predictive distribution. Third, in the extreme risk-sensitive limit, the criterion reduces to a minimax codelength objective. After centering codelengths by the oracle maximum likelihood code, the corresponding regret objective is the minimax-regret criterion whose solution is normalized maximum likelihood. In this sense, entropic SMML provides a single information-theoretic framework interpolating continuously between Bayesian MML, PAC–Bayes- type robust risk minimization, and minimax MDL. The geometric motivation for this construction comes from recent asymptotic results for ordinary SMML in regular parametric models [ 16 ]. The present paper shows that this information-geometric structure is preserved under the entropic deformation, even though the coding criterion is altered. We introduce entropic SMML , a one-parameter generalization of strict MML that interpolates between Bayesian average-case coding and worst-case codelength control. We establish a variational and PAC–Bayesian characterization of the entropic criterion, clarify its endpoint behavior, and develop a joint n–τ asymptotic theory describing when entropic SMML behaves like ordinary SMML and when it reduces to a worst-case principle. We further specialize the theory to regular exponential families, where entropic codepoints admit an interpretation as tilted Bregman centroids [ 17 ]. From an information-geometric perspective, entropic SMML applies the same m-projection as ordinary SMML after an exponential ( e-geodesic) tilting of the predictive distribution, explaining both the stability of the partition structure and the smooth recovery of ordinary SMML as τ ↓ 0.The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. ## 4 SMML, Worst-Case Codelength, and Normalized Maximum Likelihood 

This section establishes the two endpoint limits of the entropic SMML criterion. We show first that, as τ ↓ 0, entropic SMML reduces to the ordinary expected-codelength criterion of strict minimum message length. We then show that, as 

τ → ∞ , the objective converges to a worst-case codelength criterion. Since NML is characterized by minimax regret rather than by minimax absolute codelength, the precise connection to NML is obtained by applying the same soft-max limit to regret relative to the oracle maximum likelihood code. This is the classical setting of minimax-regret coding, as formalized by Shtarkov [19] and developed further by Rissanen [9] and Grünwald [10]. Remark 1 The distinction between In,τ and Jn,τ is essential. The former applies the entropic soft maximum to absolute codelengths ΛP,q,θ (x), and therefore converges to a worst-case absolute codelength criterion. NML, by contrast, is characterized by minimax regret relative to the oracle maximum likelihood codelength. The regret-centered objective Jn,τ makes this centering explicit. 

Theorems 2 and 3 show that entropic SMML interpolates between Bayesian SMML at τ = 0 and worst-case codelength coding as τ → ∞ . The regret-centered version of the same limiting construction recovers the NML minimax-regret principle, motivating the joint n–τ asymptotic analysis that follows. When the Shtarkov sum in (34) is infinite, ordinary NML is not defined. In such cases, refined MDL often replaces NML by restricted, conditional, or luckiness-weighted variants; the latter introduce a nonnegative weight on the parameter space and lead to luckiness-normalized maximum likelihood (LNML) codes [ 10 , 20 , 11 ]. ## 7 Conclusion 

We introduced entropic SMML, a risk-sensitive generalization of strict minimum message length that replaces expected codelength under the prior predictive distribution with an exponential certainty equivalent. This yields a single criterion that interpolates smoothly between Bayesian average-case coding and worst-case codelength coding: ordinary SMML is recovered as τ ↓ 0, while the corresponding regret-centered limit recovers normalized maximum likelihood as τ → ∞ .The entropic formulation admits a KL-regularized variational representation and a PAC–Bayesian-type interpretation, positioning entropic SMML at the intersection of MML, PAC–Bayes, and MDL. We established a joint n–τ asymptotic theory showing that, when the relevant fluctuation and soft-max scales are logarithmic, the average-case SMML regime holds for τn log n → 0, whereas the worst-case regime holds for τn/ log n → ∞ . For regular exponential families, partitions remain affine in sufficient-statistic space and codepoints are tilted Bregman centroids. Information-geometrically, entropic SMML applies an m-projection after an exponential ( e-geodesic) tilt, explaining the stability under curvature and the smooth recovery of ordinary SMML. Several directions remain open, including global geometric characterizations beyond fixed codebooks, broader divergence-based deformations, and non-asymptotic finite-blocklength bounds.
