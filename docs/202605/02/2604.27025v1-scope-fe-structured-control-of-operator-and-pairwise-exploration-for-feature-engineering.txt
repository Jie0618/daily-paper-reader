## SCOPE-FE: Structured Control of Operator and Pairwise Exploration for Feature Engineering Minhee Park Seongyeon Son Yonghyun Lee Eunchan Kim 

Abstract 

Automatic feature engineering is an effective approach for improving predictive performance in tabular learning. How-ever, expand-and-reduce methods, such as OpenFE, be-come increasingly computationally expensive as the input dimensionality grows. This limitation arises primarily from the combinatorial explosion of candidate features generated through operator–feature combinations. To address this is-sue, we propose SCOPE-FE, a structured search space con-trol framework that improves efficiency by reducing the can-didate space prior to feature generation. SCOPE-FE jointly regulates two major sources of combinatorial growth: the operator space and feature-pair space. First, Operator-Probing estimates the dataset-specific utility of candidate operators and eliminates low-contribution operators in ad-vance. Second, FeatureClustering employs spectral embed-ding and fuzzy c-means clustering to group structurally re-lated features, thereby restricting candidate generation to relevant within-cluster combinations. In addition, we intro-duce ReliabilityScoring, which incorporates variance across subsamples to stabilize pruning decisions. Experiments on ten benchmark datasets demonstrate that SCOPE-FE sub-stantially reduces feature engineering time while maintain-ing competitive predictive performance relative to existing baselines. The efficiency gains are particularly pronounced for high-dimensional datasets. These results indicate that structured control of the search space is an effective strat-egy for scalable automatic feature engineering. The code will be made publicly available upon acceptance. 

Keywords: Feature Generation, Automated Feature Engineering, Search Space Reduction, Tabular Data 

1 Introduction 

Automatic feature engineering (AutoFE) enhances tab-ular learning by automatically generating candidate fea-tures from the original inputs and selecting those that improve downstream predictive performance, without relying on manual feature design [1, 2, 3]. As a re-sult, AutoFE has been widely studied as an effective approach to improve tabular models while reducing the human effort required for feature construction. Existing AutoFE methods largely follow an expand-and-reduce framework, in which a large pool of can-didate features is generated using predefined operators, and retains only the most useful features through sub-sequent pruning [4, 5, 6]. Among recent approaches, OpenFE [7] is a representative method that gener-ates candidates from a predefined operator set, evalu-ates each candidate’s incremental utility via Feature-Boost, and progressively prunes the pool for down-stream tasks. This design achieves strong predictive performance without requiring retraining for each gen-erated feature set or incurring the substantial opti-mization costs commonly associated with reinforcement learning or neural network–based search methods [8, 9]. Although OpenFE improves predictive performance compared with prior work, it does not fully address the fundamental inefficiencies of broad candidate gen-eration. Its pipeline combines features and operators at a large scale in the initial stage and makes most deci-sions only after a large number of candidates have been generated. Thus, OpenFE is effective during the prun-ing phase but remains relatively permissive during the expansion phase. The total cost of feature engineering depends not only on the number of features ultimately selected but also on the number of candidates gener-ated, evaluated, and discarded throughout the process. As the number of original features or operators increase, the candidate space grows rapidly, substantially increas-ing the computational cost of both expansion and reduc-tion [10, 11, 12]. To address this limitation, we propose SCOPE-FE, a structured search-space control framework that restricts the candidate space of OpenFE, thereby making can-didate generation more selective and efficient without substantially compromising predictive performance. By preventing potentially unnecessary or invalid candidates from being generated, SCOPE-FE reduces not only the number of features passed to subsequent pruning stages but also the overall runtime of the feature generation pipeline. This is particularly important for large-scale tabular datasets. SCOPE-FE improves the efficiency of OpenFE while maintaining the stability of candidate evaluation by jointly reducing the candidate space and enhancing the reliability of early-stage scoring. The first core com-ponent is a pair-space reduction mechanism based on soft clustering. By representing each input feature us-ing fuzzy cluster memberships [13], SCOPE-FE excludes feature pairs that do not share any clusters from candi-date generation. The second component is operator-aware filtering. Before executing the full OpenFE pipeline, a lightweight probing stage is applied to each

The third component, ReliabilityScoring, mitigates the noise sensitivity of single-split evaluations by repeatedly scoring candidates across multiple subsamples and pe-nalizing those with high variance. The contributions of this study are summarized as follows: 

• We identify unconstrained candidate expansion in OpenFE as a key bottleneck and reformulate the problem as search-space control prior to candidate generation. 

• We propose FeatureClustering and OperatorProb-ing to jointly restrict the pairing and operator spa-ces before candidate generation, and introduce Re-liabilityScoring to stabilize early-stage pruning. 

• Experiments on 10 benchmark datasets show that SCOPE-FE substantially reduces runtime while maintaining competitive predictive performance, with gains most pronounced for high-dimensional datasets. 

2 Related Work 2.1 Expand-and-Reduce-Based AutoFE Aut-oFE has been widely studied to improve predictive performance on tabular data, with the expand-and-reduce paradigm serving as a representative framework [11, 7, 14]. FCTree [15] constructs features from decision tree splits but limits feature diversity. SAFE [16] extracts linear transformations from boosting trees without considering arithmetic interactions. AutoCross [17] generates feature crosses via beam search but shows limited applicability to dense numerical data. FETCH [3] learns a transferable feature engineering policy but does not explicitly reduce the candidate space and incurs substantial computational cost. OpenFE [7] implements this paradigm using unary, binary, and group-by operators, along with successive halving and FeatureBoost-based selection; however, its enumeration-based generation produces O(d2 · | O|)

candidates, resulting in significant computational overhead. 

2.2 Search-Space Reduction in Feature Gen-eration To address the combinatorial explosion, prior work has explored beam search [17], reinforcement learning [18, 19], and neural network–based approaches [20, 21] to improve search efficiency. Katz et al. (2016) proposed a meta-feature-based classifier to pre-dict feature usefulness, thereby partially reducing the search space [1]. To further constrain the search space, prior studies have leveraged feature dependencies for guided pairing [22], correlation-based filtering with pair-wise evaluation [23], clustering-based feature grouping [24, 25, 26], and interaction strength estimation [27, 28]. Soft clustering allows features to belong to multiple clus-ters, enabling more flexible modeling of overlapping re-lationships [29, 30] and addressing the rigidity of hard clustering approaches. More recently, SMARTFEAT [31] focuses on operator subset selection, whereas CN2-MCI [32] selects useful feature pairs prior to applying constructive operators. However, these methods do not jointly control both the operator and feature-pair spa-ces, which is the key contribution of our work.

4 Our Approach 

To address the computational challenges described above, we propose three components. We reduce the candidate feature space A(T, O ) at generation time using two complementary mechanisms. First, Fea-tureClustering partitions features into groups of related variables and restricts binary operators to intra-cluster pairs. Second, OperatorProbing preselects a compact set of effective operators via subsampled evaluation, dis-carding those that contribute little to predictive per-formance. Finally, we stabilize the candidate selection process using ReliabilityScoring . The standard OpenFE pipeline evaluates each candidate once using Feature-Boost on a single data split, which makes it susceptible to sampling noise. To address this limitation, we replace the scoring function in the first round of the successive-halving procedure with a reliability score based on a lower-confidence-bound-style criterion. Each candidate is evaluated on multiple random subsamples, and fea-tures with high variance across evaluations are penal-ized. 4.2 Pair-space Scope Control The original OpenFE framework constructs candidate features by exhaustively pairing each feature in T with every other feature for each binary operator in O. For a dataset with d features and p binary operators, this results in 

O(p · d2) candidate features, which is computationally prohibitive for large datasets. We observe that many of these feature pairs are unrelated, producing candidates that do not improve predictive performance. To address this issue, we propose FeatureClustering , which partitions T into groups of related features and restricts binary operators to intra-cluster pairs. We consider two clustering strategies: hard clustering (Section 4.2.2), which assigns each feature to exactly one cluster, and soft clustering (Section 4.2.3), which allows each feature to belong to multiple clusters with varying degrees of membership. 

4.2.1 Similarity Measures To cluster the features, we first quantify pairwise similarity. Given d features, we construct a d × d symmetric similarity matrix S,where each entry Sij measures the statistical association between features ti and tj . Because different feature types require different association measures, we employ three statistics depending on the feature-type pair: absolute Pearson correlation for numeric–numeric pairs, bias-corrected Cramér’s V for categorical–categorical pairs, and eta-squared ( η2) for mixed-type pairs. These measures are combined to form the symmetric similarity matrix S. Appendix B provides the formal definitions of these measures. Figure 4.1: SCOPE-FE pipeline overview. Given input data, SCOPE-constrained expansion applies operator-space control (OperatorProbing) and pair-space control (FeatureClustering) to limit candidate generation. The resulting features are pruned via reliability-aware successive halving with stability-adjusted scoring, and the selected features are then used for importance evaluation and final model training.

4.5 Theoretical Analysis Starting from OpenFE’s 

O(p · d2) candidate space (Eq. (3.3)), SCOPE-FE re-duces this space through two independent mechanisms. 

FeatureClustering (Section 4.2) partitions the d features into K = ⌈d/τ ⌉ clusters with a target size of τ , and re-stricts binary operators to intra-cluster pairs. This re-duces the number of admissible pairs per operator from 

O(d2) to O(K · τ 2) = O(d · τ ).Separately, operator-space control (Section 4.3) retains only the top Ntop operators from the original set of size p, reducing the operator space by a factor of 

Ntop /p . This selection applies to both unary and binary operators, whereas clustering-based reduction targets only binary operators. By combining both mechanisms, the number of candidate features generated by binary operators in SCOPE-FE becomes: (4.13) |A binary SCOPE-FE | ≈ Ntop · O (d · τ )

The overall reduction ratio for the binary-dominated candidate space is: (4.14) |A SCOPE-FE ||A OpenFE | ≈ Ntop 

p × τd

Because Ntop ≪ p and τ ≪ d in typical settings, these factors combine multiplicatively to yield a substantial reduction. This effect is particularly pronounced for high-dimensional datasets, where large d values cause the τ /d term to significantly shrink the quadratic pair space. The reduction propagates to the evaluation stage: because successive halving evaluates all gener-ated candidates using FeatureBoost, a smaller candidate pool directly reduces the number of evaluations in each round. Consequently, runtime savings are compounded across both the generation and selection stages of the pipeline. 

5 Experiment 5.1 Datasets and Evaluation Protocol In this study, we evaluated the same ten public benchmark datasets used in OpenFE (Zhang et al., 2023): three re-gression tasks (California Housing, Medical, and Micro-soft), five binary classification tasks (Diabetes, Nomao, Vehicle, Broken Machine, and Telecom), and two mul-ticlass classification tasks (Jannis and Covertype). To ensure a fair comparison, we adopted experimental set-tings identical to those of OpenFE, including the num-ber of folds, the number of data blocks, and the number of generated features. For each random seed, Light-GBM was trained independently on the same dataset, and the final performance was reported as the mean and standard deviation over ten runs. The evaluation metrics were RMSE for regression, ROC-AUC for bi-nary classification, and accuracy for multiclass classi-fication. All experiments were conducted on a Mac-Book Air equipped with an Apple M4 chip and 24 GB of RAM. 

5.2 Baseline Comparison We compared SCOPE-FE with six baselines: the original feature set (base) and five AutoFE methods, including FCTree [15], SAFE [16], AutoCross [17], FETCH [3], and OpenFE [7]. 

5.3 Main Results As shown in Table 5.1, SCOPE-FE substantially reduces feature engineering runtime compared to OpenFE while maintaining competitive overall predictive performance. Runtime reductions are particularly pronounced for high-dimensional datasets, with reductions of approximately 81%, 64%, and 58% for MI, BR, and CO, respectively (up to a 5.2 × speedup on MI). These gains stem from FeatureClustering, which suppresses pair-space explosion through spectral embed-ding and FCM clustering, and OperatorProbing, which eliminates uninformative operators prior to candidate generation. Among the other baselines, FCTree, FETCH, and AutoCross exhibit substantially higher runtimes, and FETCH fails to complete within 24 hours on all three datasets. SAFE is faster but limited to a subset of task types and yields weaker predictive performance. OpenFE remains the strongest baseline in terms of bal-ancing efficiency and predictive performance, whereas SCOPE-FE consistently improves runtime with only modest changes in predictive performance. In terms of predictive performance, SCOPE-FE achieves identical scores to OpenFE on MI and NO, slightly im-proves performance on CA, DI, and JA, and shows mod-est degradation on the remaining datasets. These re-sults suggest that structured search-space control can substantially improve efficiency while preserving com-petitive predictive performance. 6 Conclusion 

In this study, we propose SCOPE-FE, a structured search-space control framework for efficient AutoFE. SCOPE-FE simultaneously constrains the operator and pairing spaces prior to candidate generation through OperatorProbing and FeatureClustering, thereby ef-fectively reducing the candidate explosion inherent in expand-and-reduce approaches such as OpenFE. In addition, ReliabilityScoring stabilizes the early-stage pruning process by accounting for variability across subsamples. Extensive experiments on 10 benchmark datasets demonstrate that SCOPE-FE significantly re-duces runtime while maintaining competitive predictive performance. In particular, the efficiency improvements are more pronounced for high-dimensional datasets, where the search space grows rapidly. Overall, these results indicate that SCOPE-FE is a practical and scal-able approach for improving the efficiency of AutoFE.
