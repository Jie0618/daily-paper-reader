## Abstract 

In this paper, we propose Lagrangian Gaussian Processes ( LGPs ) for probabilistic and data-efficient learning of dynamics via discrete forced Euler-Lagrange equa-tions. Importantly, the geometric structure of the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle, which governs the motion of dynamical systems, is preserved by construction in the absence of external forces. This allows learning physically consistent models that overcome erroneous drift in the system’s energy, thereby providing stable long-term predictions. At the core of our approach lie linear operators for Gaussian process conditioning, constructed from discrete forced Euler-Lagrange equations and variational discretization schemes. Thereby and unlike prior work, the method enables learning dynamics from discrete position snapshots , i. e., without access to a system’s velocities or momenta. This is particularly relevant for a large class of practical scenarios where only position measurements are available, for instance, in motion capture or visual servoing applications. We demonstrate the data-efficiency and generalization capabilities of the LGPs in various synthetic and real-world case studies, including a real-world soft robot with hysteresis. The experimental results underscore that the LGPs learn physically consistent dynamics with uncer-tainty quantification solely from sparse positional data and enable stable long-term predictions. 

## 1 Introduction 

Exploiting known physical principles when learning dynamics models has become a key strategy for improving data efficiency and generalization [ 1]. In this regard, learning physically consistent models from data can enable the use of well-known, reliable model-based planning or control in complex real-world applications, such as fluid mechanics [2] or soft robotics [3, 4]. A powerful approach to enable efficient learning without losing expressivity is to incorporate only a very general system understanding. One example of such non-restrictive prior knowledge is the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle, which governs the motion of dynamical systems via a foundational energy law. Seminal work on so-called Lagrangian Neural Networks ( LNNs ) [ 5] or Hamiltonian Neural Networks ( HNNs ) [ 6 ] leverage this energy principle as algebraic inductive biases to enforce hard physical constraints at minimal computational cost. This approach contrasts with sampling-heavy collocation methods for physics-informed learning [2, 7]. Recent work extends [ 5, 6 ] to enable energy-consistent learning in practically relevant scenarios [ 3, 8]by considering non-canonical coordinates [ 9] or non-conservative systems [ 10 ], i. e., systems with energy dissipation and/or control inputs. However, this prior work is limited in three directions: (i)

First , providing physically consistent long-term predictions—without erroneous drift of the system’s energy—needs special attention. Namely, the dynamics learning and prediction scheme needs to preserve the geometric structure of the underlying energy principle [ 11 ]. In this regard, most existing work requires specific symplectic integration schemes or ignores structure preservation altogether. 

Second , an uncertainty quantification is highly relevant for various applications, such as stochastic control or safe learning [ 12 ]. However, the few works that propose learning mechanisms for preserving the geometric structure of the energy principle do not provide probabilistic dynamics models [ 13 , 14 ]. 

Third , the vast majority of existing work on energy-consistent learning relies on measurements of the entire system state [ 15 –17 ]. This means that, e. g., beyond position snapshots, momentum or velocity measurements are required for training, which is a restrictive assumption in many practical settings. In this paper, we propose structure-preserving Lagrangian Gaussian Processes ( LGPs ) for learning probabilistic non-conservative dynamics models. We stress that the LGPs do not require system-specific prior knowledge but use only the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle. By harnessing discrete forced Euler-Lagrange linear operators, we are able to learn the GPs only from position data, without requiring restrictive velocity or momentum measurements (see Figure 1). We contextualize our approach to related work in Section 5, and present an overview in Table 1. Our contributions are: 

(a) Two structure-preserving LGP schemes, a discrete and a continuous version, for probabilistic learning of dynamics models, characterized by the system’s Lagrangian and external force functions. 

(b) A validation in various synthetic and real-world systems, including a controlled double pendulum and a controlled pneumatic soft robot. Specifically, both the discrete and continuous LGPs preserve the symplectic structure of the underlying energy principle, thereby enabling energy conservation and stable long-term predictions. By using a normalization condition, our approach is not restricted to a fixed kernel choice, unlike most prior work. Yet, if additional knowledge about the energy model is available, it can be incorporated into the GP kernel to facilitate learning. The continuous LGP additionally generalizes to custom prediction time-step widths and allows constructing functions for linear observables of the learned Lagrangian, such as the Hamiltonian or the conjugate momentum.

In summary , the discrete and continuous LGPs preserve the geometric structure of the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle by construction of the linear operators and, thereby, enable prediction without erroneous energy drift. Specifically, the continuous scheme allows for prediction at custom step sizes by embedding a variational discretization. Exploiting discrete forced Euler-Lagrange equations, our approach learns only from position snapshots q, without requiring momentum or velocity measurements ˙q. The full methodological details are given in Appendix A, which includes approaches for incorporating further physics knowledge about L and F . Equipped with these properties, the proposed LGPs are well-suited to provide stable, physically consistent long-term predictions in complex practical applications, which we test in the following.

Figure 2a illustrates how additional physics information in the kernel choice can improve the accuracy of the learned Lagrangian and force GPs in terms of their posterior mean and covariance. We highlight that—without further assumptions—the Lagrangian and external force function of a system cannot be determined uniquely from trajectory data [ 21 ]. Instead, a single trajectory can be generated from different equivalent or alternative Lagrangians [ 21 ]. In this light, the LGPs learn the true Lagrangian up to a scaling operation, under certain conditions detailed in Appendix A.5. Regardless, the LGPs 

provide a valid Euler-Lagrange operator, useful for prediction tasks. A quantitative study of such predictions with varying system dimensions nq and varying amounts of training data N shows that the LGPs outperform the standard GP (see Figure 2b and Appendix C.1). Moreover, incorporating a physics-inspired kernel in the LGPs improves the predictive performance and the generalization beyond the training domain further (see Appendix A.5). By using variational discretization schemes in Section 3, the learned LGPs preserve the symplectic structure of the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle (1) by construction. In practice, this means that a set of initial conditions preserves its volume if predicted forward in time. In turn, this provides—in a conservative system, i. e., without external forces—an approximately constant energy level of the forward predictions. In fact, it oscillates around the initial energy, corresponding to constant-volume deformations of the set of initial conditions. We observe this property in Figure 3a and Appendix C.2, where we validate in a conservative pendulum that the structure-preserving LGPs yield accurate long-term predictions without energy drift, unlike the standard GP . 4.2 Task 2: Controlled Real-World Double Pendulum 

Second, we evaluate the prediction performance with a real-world double pendulum data set [ 27 ]. The pendulum features angular positions q and is non-conservative in two ways. First, the joints are driven by a torque input u, and second, the pendulum is subject to friction, which dissipates energy over time. For training, we only use N = 300 position and input torque data triples, randomly sampled from the noisy trajectory measurements. For testing, we choose two 5 s -prediction scenarios, one with non-zero motor torque inputs u and one without inputs, i. e., dissipation only. The predictions in Figures 4 and A10 underscore the capability of the structure-preserving LGPs to perform accurate long-term predictions, in contrast to the baseline GP . Importantly, the learned LGPs yield a monotonically decreasing system energy (Hamiltonian H) in the absence of control inputs, which is consistent with the dissipative nature of the pendulum. We stress that the continuous LGP enables stable predictions at larger time step sizes , i. e., ∆ttrain < ∆tpred , while staying on par regarding the prediction error. 

4.3 Task 3: Controlled Real-World Soft Robot 

The previous test scenarios featured comparably obvious generalized coordinates, i. e., the positions 

q, that clearly describe the dynamics to be learned. In contrast, the controlled real-world soft robot in Figure 5 exhibits—besides highly nonlinear dynamics—no straightforward “position coordinates.” In-stead, we perform training and prediction based on so-called “shape-parameters” q⊤ = [∆ x, ∆y, δℓ ]

[29 ], obtained via a motion tracking set-up [ 30 ]. Notably, the robot’s silicon structure is non-rigid and is driven by three pneumatic pressure inputs u, which together yield complex, non-conservative dy-namics with hysteresis. Despite the challenging system behavior and the unknown system dimension, the LGPs provide accurate long-term predictions, unlike the standard GP .85 Related Work 

Energy-consistent dynamics learning. Integrating continuous-time dynamics into learning-based models is a popular and related approach. Early work in this direction learns vector-valued flow maps that determine the evolution of a system’s state, e. g., using neural ODEs [ 31 ], but does not incorporate stronger inductive physics biases. In contrast, various recent work computes a flow map from an approximation of a system’s Hamiltonian [ 6, 15 , 18 , 19 , 32 –38 , 38 –41 ] or Lagrangian [ 5, 9, 10 , 16 , 17 , 20 – 22 , 26 , 42 ], which reflect the exchange of energy within the system. Most of these works apply continuous Euler-Lagrange or Hamiltonian mechanics equations—induced by the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle (1) —to the approximated Hamiltonian/Lagrangian for constructing the flow map [ 5 , 6, 9 , 10 , 15 – 19 , 22 , 26 , 32 –36 , 38 –42 ]. This usually requires access to momentum or velocity measurements, which is a restrictive assumption in many practical settings [ 13 ]. While velocity and momentum estimates can be approximated from position measurements via numerical differentiation, this approach often introduces significant inaccuracies due to noise [ 43 ]. In contrast, we circumvent these issues via discrete Euler-Lagrange equations (7) , which only require positional data [ 13 , 20 , 21 , 44 , 45 ]. In addition, existing energy-consistent learning methods—unlike the proposed schemes—often rely on strong assumptions about the kernel design, such as a quadratic kinetic energy, to ensure regularity of the learned Lagrangian [17, 22]. 

Structure preservation of energy principles. When simulating dynamics from a learned flow map, special attention is required regarding the underlying symplectic structure. Specifically, for a dynamical system with system energy (Hamiltonian) H, its variational symmetries yield conserved quantities of the flow, such as energy conservation, by Noether’s theorem. Various works require symplectic integration schemes to preserve this structure, as they utilize continuous Euler-Lagrange equations, or even ignore structure preservation altogether, causing the learned model to violate the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle [ 5 , 9, 15 – 17 , 22 , 25 , 26 ]. The proposed methods differ from most of these approaches by employing discrete forced Euler-Lagrange equations (7) that preserve— in the absence of external forces—the symplectic structure of the underlying energy principle by construction [ 13 , 20 , 21 , 46 ]. Considering the continuous LGP in Section 3.2, the symplectic structure is implicitly preserved as all data enters the GP through a variational integrator, embedded in the linear operators LDI and FDI . This procedure relates to [ 20 ], where a similar approach is applied to the GP ’s posterior. 

Learning dynamics with dissipation and inputs. Seminal works on Lagrangian or Hamiltonian-based models [ 5, 6 , 9 ] focused on conservative dynamical systems, meaning that no energy crosses the system boundary, e. g., via dissipation or inputs. Recent extensions enable learning with such forcing terms [ 10 , 13 – 16 , 18 , 25 , 33 , 42 ], enabling practical implementation in several practically relevant modeling problems, for instance, in robotics [ 3, 8 , 14 ]. Considering existing approaches that enable learning from position data, most work [ 20 , 21 , 44 ] is restricted to conservative systems by building on the principle of least action, i. e., the first term in (1) . Instead, the proposed LGPs rely on the entire Lagrange-d’Alembert principle (1), which admits learning non-conservative dynamics. 

Uncertainty quantification. Considering methods for energy-based dynamics learning, a sig-nificant part of the literature focuses on deterministic neural networks, such as LNNs or HNNs 

[ 5, 6, 9, 10 , 13 , 14 , 32 – 34 , 42 , 45 ]. In contrast, schemes providing an uncertainty-quantification are almost exclusively based on GPs [15 – 22 , 26 , 35 , 36 , 38 – 41 ]. To the best of our knowledge, the proposed LGPs are the first methods to enable learning of non-conservative probabilistic dynamics models from position data. 

## 6 Discussion 

Limitations. The proposed LGPs inherit the typical limitations and properties of GPs , including their limited scalability with the number of training data points. Thus, future work may consider incorporating sparse GP approaches. Considering the required prior knowledge to model dynamics using LGPs , we acknowledge that it is necessary to assume a system dimension. Yet, we have shown in a real-world soft robot—whose actual spatial coordinates are continuous and thus infinite-dimensional—that the LGPs model performs well even if the true system dimension is not matched. Moreover, while we test on noisy real-world data, we are aware that the considered midpoint rule 9for variational discretization may be sensitive to higher levels of sensor noise. An effective ad hoc countermeasure is to raise the signal-to-noise ratio by increasing the training time step size. However, this comes at the cost of potentially losing high-frequency features. In this regard, future work may investigate the effect of other variational integration schemes. 

Conclusion. In this paper, we propose Lagrangian Gaussian Processes ( LGPs ) for learning proba-bilistic, non-conservative dynamics models. Both presented schemes, discrete and continuous LGP ,preserve the geometric structure of the underlying Lagrange-d’Alembert principle by construction, in the unforced case. By conditioning on discrete forced Euler-Lagrange equations, the LGPs learn 

only from position data . To the best of our knowledge, the proposed LGPs enable, for the first time, learning non-conservative dynamics from position data while providing an uncertainty quantification. While our approach enables learning with generic kernels, adding physics knowledge into kernel design improves data efficiency, generalization, and predictive performance. Various synthetic and real-world case studies—including a pneumatic real-world soft robot—show that the LGPs yield highly accurate, physically consistent long-term predictions in complex real-world applications.

Ambiguity in unforced Euler-Lagrange equations. The reason for this ambiguity is that the normalization conditions in Section 3 rule out degenerate Lagrangians, but do not restrict learning to the exact shape of the true Lagrangian. For unforced systems, [ 21 , Section 2.1.2] characterizes the residual non-uniqueness via the equivalence class 

˜L(q, ˙q) = ρ L (q, ˙q) + c + ˙ q⊤∇W (q) , (A22) parameterized by a continuously differentiable function W : Rnq → R and constants ρ ∈ R \ { 0},

c ∈ R, provided that there does not exist an alternative Lagrangian that explains the data. For the Hamiltonian, (A22) induces 

H[ ˜L]( q, ˙q) = ρ H[L]( q, ˙q) − c , (A23) according to [21, Lemma 2.3]. Similar results for other linear observables and discrete Lagrangians are given in [21]. 

Ambiguity in forced Euler-Lagrange equations. However, the equivalence class (A22) alone does not capture all ambiguous solutions in our forced setting. In particular, we face two challenges: vFirst, the data that is generated by a system that obeys the forced Euler-Lagrange equations 

ddt ∇ ˙q L(q, ˙q) − ∇ q L(q, ˙q) = F (u, q, ˙q) , (A24) and second, that the Lagrangian and external force are learned jointly . In this case, for any continu-ously differentiable Φ : Rnq → R, the modified pair (˚L, ˚F ), that is 

˚L(q, ˙q) = L(q, ˙q) + Φ( q) , ˚F (u, q, ˙q) = F (u, q, ˙q) − ∇ q Φ( q) , (A25) yields the same trajectories as (L, F ) under (A24) , since both sides of (A24) can be shifted by −∇ q Φ

while still satisfying the quality. For mechanical Lagrangians L = T − V , the choice Φ( q) = 2 V (q) in (A25) maps the true Lagrangian onto its energy, 

˚L(q, ˙q) = T (q, ˙q) + V (q) , ˚F (x) = F (x) − 2∇q V (q) , (A26) which numerically coincides with H[L], while the corresponding Hamiltonian satisfies H[˚L] = 

T −V = L. In numerical studies, such as in Figure A2a, we observed this swap of learned Lagrangian and Hamiltonian multiple times when the force kernel was flexible enough to absorb −2∇q V .To re-establish that the learned Lagrangians lie within the equivalence class (A22) , additional 

restrictions on the employed kernels are required. One example, which we use in this paper, is to prevent the force from absorbing terms of the form ∇q Φ by employing a physics-informed kernel design, based on (A21) . The effect of a linear force kernel can be seen in Figure A2b, where the learned quantities comply with the equivalence class (A22) for some ρ < 0. C.1 Extended Quantitative Study with Multi-Link Pendulum 

In this appendix, we give the full quantitative study with multi-body pendulum simulations. Specif-ically, we consider a standard GP and discrete/continuous LGPs with generic and physics-inspired 

kernels in controlled single, double, and triple pendulums, i. e., nq ∈ { 1, 2, 3}. We train all methods at the training and prediction step size ∆ttrain = ∆ tpred = 5 · 10 −2 for different training data budgets 

N ∈ { 25 , 50 , 100 , 200 , 300 }. For evaluation, we use the Root Mean Squared Error ( RMSE ) between a ground-truth 20 -step simulation and the corresponding learning-based predictions. The results of the quantitative study are visualized in Figure A8. As expected, adding prior knowledge about the data-generating dynamics improves the predictive performance. First, we see that the proposed LGPs outperform the standard GP predictions across dimensions. Second, the discrete and continuous LGPs with physics-inspired kernel provide more accurate predictions than LGPs with generic kernels. This methodological characteristic is promising, as the employed knowledge that an embodied system obeys the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle is very general, meaning that only 

few system-specific expert knowledge is required. Therefore, the LGPs provide a vastly improved predictive performance essentially for free for the considered system class. Given extremely sparse data, the LGPs achieve performance comparable to the standard GP baseline. The improvement relative to standard GPs is particularly evident for system dimensions nq > 1 and from approximately N = 100 data points. In this regime, we observe that LGPs trained with N = 100 

data points perform on par or better than a standard GP trained on three times more data points (see Figure A8 for nq = 2 ). C.2 Structure Preservation in Long-term Predictions 

The proposed LGPs preserve the geometric structure of the Lagrange-d’Alembert principle by con-struction in the absence of external forces. We illustrate this property in Figure 3a and give further details on the implications for long-term predictions in this appendix. Specifically, we simulate a conservative single pendulum, i. e., with zero inputs and damping coefficient b = 0 , forward for 30 

seconds at time step width ∆ttrain = ∆ tpred = 5 · 10 −2 and evaluate the predictive performance of different learning-based dynamics models. In Figure A9, we compare the long-term forward predic-tions of LGPs and standard GP in terms of their position coordinate predictions q and the associated system energy H. To construct the current system energy H, we evaluate the true Hamiltonian at the predicted trajectory samples. In the example, the LGPs use a physics-inspired kernel. The evaluation in Figure A9 shows that the LGPs yield accurate and stable long-term predictions with a system energy that oscillates around a constant level. As discussed in Section 4.1, the oscillation is characteristic of the employed variational integration procedure and occurs in the ground truth solution as well. In contrast, the standard GP position predictions diverge, leading to a physically inconsistent accumulation of system energy over time.
