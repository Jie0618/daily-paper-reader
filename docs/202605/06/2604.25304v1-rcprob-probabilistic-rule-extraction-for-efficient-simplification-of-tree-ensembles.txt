Abstract 

Tree ensembles are widely used in industrial machine learning due to their strong pre-dictive performance and efficient training procedures. However, as the number of trees in an ensemble grows, the resulting models become increasingly difficult for humans to inter-pret. To address this limitation, explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) studies methods that generate interpretable models capable of explaining complex predictors. One approach consists of extracting decision rules from tree ensembles while attempting to preserve the predictive performance of the original model. In previous work, we introduced RuleCOSI+, a greedy heuristic algorithm for extracting compact rule-based models from tree ensembles. Although RuleCOSI+ produces accurate and interpretable rule sets, it relies on repeated empirical frequency counting over the training data to estimate rule confidence, which be-comes computationally expensive for large datasets. In this paper, we propose RCProb, a probabilistic reformulation of RuleCOSI+ designed to reduce the computational cost of rule extraction. RCProb estimates rule statistics using Dirichlet-smoothed class priors and Beta-smoothed condition likelihoods combined through a Naive Bayes formulation, avoiding repeated dataset scans. Experiments on 33 benchmark datasets show that RCProb main-tains competitive predictive performance while reducing runtime by approximately 22 ×

compared with RuleCOSI+, while producing more compact rule sets on average. 

# 1 Introduction 

Tree ensemble learning methods, such as Random Forests (RF) and Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM), have become dominant predictive models due to their strong empirical performance and robustness. They are widely deployed in high-impact domains including manufacturing quality monitoring, credit risk assessment, fraud detection, and medical decision support [1]. Despite their predictive strength, ensemble models grow increasingly complex as the number of trees and splits increases, reducing interpretability and limiting adoption in applications where transparency and accountability are critical. This limitation has motivated extensive research on post-hoc explainability for tree ensem-bles [2, 3]. Rule extraction techniques are particularly attractive because they transform ensem-bles into human-readable IF–THEN rules that explicitly describe decision regions. Several ap-proaches have been proposed. RuleFit (RFIT) [4] converts tree leaves into rules and fits a sparse linear model for selection, often producing accurate but large rulesets. The inTrees framework (IT) [5] extracts and prunes rules to construct a simplified learner, while DefragTrees (DFT) [6] adopts a Bayesian model selection perspective to approximate ensembles with compact rule sets. Forest-based trees (FBTs) [7, 8] generate a single decision tree surrogate, improving struc-tural simplicity at the cost of potentially large tree size. Optimization-driven approaches [9–11] further attempt to explicitly control rule size, coverage, and overlap, but may incur substantial computational cost. 1

Probabilistic rule learners have also been proposed to model uncertainty in rule-based clas-sifiers. However, these approaches are typically designed either as standalone probabilistic classifiers or as leaf-level smoothing procedures, rather than as uncertainty-aware simplification mechanisms integrated into greedy ensemble compression. In earlier work, we introduced RuleCOSI (RC) [12], a greedy rule combination and simplifi-cation framework for extracting interpretable rules from boosted ensembles in imbalanced set-tings. RC merges and prunes rules using pessimistic error estimates to obtain compact decision lists. This framework was later generalized to RuleCOSI+ (RC+) [13], extending applicability to both bagging and boosting ensembles and improving scalability. RC+ demonstrated that greedy rule combination guided by coverage and confidence constraints can produce concise, non-redundant rulesets that closely approximate ensemble behavior. However, both RC and RC+ rely on repeated empirical counting over the training data to compute rule coverage, confidence, and generalization estimates during the greedy search pro-cess. As ensemble size and dataset scale increase, these repeated scans become a computational bottleneck. Additionallyl, recent probabilistic smoothing techniques have been explored to reg-ularize tree predictions and improve robustness, such as post-hoc Beta-Binomial leaf smoothing [14]. In this paper, we introduce RCProb , a Bayesian-smoothed rule evaluation extension of RC+. RCProb replaces repeated count-based rule evaluation with conjugate probabilistic inference. Instead of estimating rule confidence via empirical frequencies, RCProb models rule posteri-ors using Dirichlet-smoothed class priors and Beta-smoothed condition likelihoods aggregated through a Naive Bayes formulation. This additive evidence structure enables efficient evaluation of merged rules without repeated data scans. Additionally, RCProb incorporates leaf-level class distributions from the original ensemble as informative priors, enabling support-dependent pos-terior shrinkage for low-coverage rules. By shifting rule evaluation from empirical counting to posterior inference, RCProb substantially reduces computational overhead during rule merging and pruning while preserving predictive performance. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

• We propose RCProb, a probabilistic rule extraction framework that extends RC+ by re-placing count-based rule evaluation with posterior class probability modeling using Dirich-let and Beta-smoothed estimates. 

• We introduce an efficient atomic-condition inference mechanism with additive evidence reuse and leaf-informed Dirichlet smoothing, enabling rule merging and pruning without repeated data scans. 

• We empirically demonstrate that RCProb reduces computational cost during rule sim-plification while maintaining predictive performance and producing more compact rule sets. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on rule extraction and ensemble explainability. Section 3 introduces the necessary preliminaries and notation. Section 4 presents the RCProb methodology in detail. Section 5 describes the experimental design, followed by results analysis and discussion in Section 6. Finally, Section 7 concludes the paper and points out limitations and future work. 

# 2 Related Work 

The interpretation of tree-based ensemble models has been widely studied in explainable artifi-cial intelligence (XAI) and knowledge discovery. Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into (i) feature-based explanation methods [15, 16] , (ii) surrogate and approximation models 2[17], and (iii) rule-based post-hoc ensemble simplification techniques. RCProb belongs to the third category and is most closely related to rule extraction and ensemble compression methods. 

2.1 Rule extraction from tree ensembles 

Rule-based post-hoc methods aim to extract IF–THEN rules that represent the decision logic of an ensemble. RuleFit [4] converts tree leaves into new features, to posteriorly fit a sparse linear model (i.e., LASSO regressor) to automatically select relevant features. RuleFit often achieves strong predictive performance but frequently produces large and redundant rule sets. The inTrees framework [5] extracts decision rules from tree ensembles and prunes them by balancing rule complexity and error rate, then further removes redundant rules using feature selection to produce a simplified tree ensemble learner. Optimization-driven approaches have further explored the trade-off between accuracy and interpretability in rule extraction from ensembles. The two-stage framework in [9], built on a Random Forest, first performs local rule extraction by simplifying individual rules to reduce the number of conditions, and then applies a global selection stage using ensemble feature selection and Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II). The multi-objective fitness function jointly minimizes predictive error and maximizes interpretability metrics, such as reducing the number of rules and conditions, with additional filtering via LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net, and chi-square methods. Similarly, [10] formulate rule selection as a mixed-integer optimization problem to construct an Optimized Rule Ensemble (ORE) that balances predictive performance, coverage, and model size (number of rules, rule length, and overlap). Starting from a Random Forest, rules are extracted and preselected based on coverage and confidence, then a constrained optimization step selects a subset that minimizes complexity and overlap while maximizing coverage and rule contribution, maintaining performance comparable to the original forest. Association rule mining is further used to refine and enrich the final rule set. Structural constraints on extracted models have also been investigated to enforce non-overlapping and compact rule representations. In [18], rules are first extracted from a Random Forest and all pairwise combinations are analyzed to measure overlap in their coverage regions. These relationships are represented as a graph in which nodes correspond to rules and edges encode non-overlapping relations. Maximal cliques are then identified, each representing a set of mutually non-overlapping rules that define distinct and interpretable decision regions. The resulting rule sets are evaluated via cross-validation to select models that balance predictive performance and structural simplicity. Likewise, [19] propose a reduction framework that com-bines heuristic search and sparsity-inducing regularization. Starting from rules extracted from tree ensembles, hill-climbing is used to iteratively refine the rule subset, while sparse group lasso further enforces compactness by shrinking and selecting rules in a structured manner. The ob-jective is to reduce the number of rules and enhance interpretability while preserving predictive accuracy. Logic-based extraction has been formulated through declarative optimization in [11], where rule extraction from ensembles is cast as an Answer Set Programming problem with global and pairwise constraints. Although optimization and logic-based approaches can produce compact rule sets, they may incur substantial computational cost when applied to large ensembles. Complementary to extraction, scalable visualization techniques have been proposed. The framework in [20] organizes extracted rules using matrix-based representations to improve in-terpretability and highlight anomalous patterns. While valuable for analysis, such methods are not primarily designed for ensemble simplification. 

2.2 Probabilistic smoothing and probabilistic rule models 

Probabilistic representations have been used to regularize predictions and improve robustness in low-support regions. Post-hoc Beta-Binomial smoothing of leaf probabilities was introduced 3in [14], improving generalization while preserving tree structure. Beyond leaf-level smoothing, fully probabilistic rule models have also been proposed. In [21], a Bayesian framework for learning interpretable rule sets was introduced, where prior dis-tributions explicitly control rule size and structure, and posterior inference is used to construct compact disjunctive rule models. Such approaches provide principled uncertainty modeling and structural regularization but are typically designed as standalone classifiers learned directly from data rather than as post-hoc simplifications of pre-trained ensemble models. While these probabilistic and Bayesian methods improve robustness and uncertainty mod-eling, they do not explicitly address scalable rule merging and simplification within greedy ensemble compression frameworks. RCProb differs by integrating conjugate probabilistic in-ference directly into the rule combination process, enabling uncertainty-aware rule evaluation, significantly reducing computation time and mantaining similar performance to RC+. 

2.3 RC, RC+ and positioning of RCProb 

RC introduced a greedy rule combination framework for simplifying boosted tree ensembles, using pessimistic error estimates and pruning to obtain compact decision lists. However, it worked only with small boosted trees [12]. RC+ later generalized the approach to both bagging and boosting ensembles and improved scalability, making it capable of working with hundreds of trees [13]. However, despite these advances, rule evaluation in RC+ relies on repeated empirical counting to compute coverage, confidence, and generalization estimates during rule merging. RCProb extends this framework by replacing count-based evaluation with conjugate probabilistic inference, enabling uncertainty-aware rule merging while reducing computational overhead.

# 4 RCProb: Probabilistic rule extraction from tree ensembles 

This section presents RCProb, a probabilistic extension of RC+ for post-hoc simplification of classification tree ensembles. RCProb retains the algorithmic foundation of RC+, includ-ing its greedy rule combination and simplification framework, while introducing probabilistic mechanisms to estimate rule coverage and confidence more efficiently during rule merging and generalization. Algorithm 1 summarizes the overall procedure. Given a trained tree ensemble H and training data D, RCProb converts each tree hk ∈ H into a set of decision rules by transforming every root-to-leaf path into a rule. Each rule consists of (i) a conjunction of atomic conditions Ar

and (ii) a class distribution derived from leaf-level statistics. 5The algorithm initializes a simplified ruleset using the rules extracted from the first tree. It then iteratively processes the remaining trees in the ensemble. At each iteration, rules from the current simplified ruleset are combined with rules extracted from the next tree, generating candidate rules by merging conditions and intersecting coverage regions. In contrast to RC+, candidate rule evaluation in RCProb relies on probabilistic estimates rather than repeated empirical dataset scans. Specifically, rule coverage is approximated using marginal condition probabilities, while rule confidence is obtained from a hybrid posterior that combines Naive Bayes evidence aggregation with leaf-informed shrinkage. This posterior de-pends on three parameters: a smoothing parameter η controlling additive smoothing of priors and likelihoods, a shrinkage parameter τ governing the strength of leaf-informed regularization, and a support-adaptive mixing parameter n0 that balances Naive Bayes and leaf-based evidence. After candidate generation, the expanded ruleset is simplified using two stages. First, se-quential covering pruning selects high-confidence rules while removing covered samples. This step retains the original empirical counting formulation of RC+. Second, rule generalization reduces rule complexity by iteratively removing conditions based on a pessimistic error estimate controlled by the confidence level c.After simplification, the resulting ruleset is compared with the previous one and the better-performing ruleset on the training data is retained. The process continues until all trees have been processed or an early stopping condition is reached. Finally, a default rule predicting the majority class of the remaining uncovered instances is appended to the simplified ruleset. RCProb therefore preserves the extract–combine–prune–generalize structure of RC+ while modifying how rule statistics are estimated during rule combination and simplification. The probabilistic formulation of the new algorithm is described in the following sections.

# 5 Experimental Design 

This section describes the protocol used to evaluate RCProb using empirical experiments.The proposed algorithm was implemented as a Python library 1 (updated upon publication), based on the scikit-learn package v1.8.0. 

5.1 Datasets 

The evaluation is conducted on 33 publicly available benchmark classification datasets span-ning diverse domains and exhibiting varying levels of dimensionality and class imbalance. The datasets differ substantially in the number of instances, number of features, and imbalance ratio (IR), providing heterogeneous evaluation conditions. A detailed summary of dataset characteristics is provided in Table 1, including the number of instances, number of attributes, class definitions, and imbalance ratio when applicable. All datasets are processed using standard preprocessing procedures. Stratified partitioning is applied during cross-validation to preserve class distributions. 

5.2 Ensemble Training 

RCProb is evaluated as a post-hoc simplification method applied to trained tree ensembles. Two representative algorithms are considered: Random Forest (RF) [22] as an independent tree ensemble learning algorithm, and Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM) [23] as the dependent ensemble learning algorithm. For each dataset, ensembles are trained using fixed hyperparame-ter configurations to ensure fair comparison across post-hoc simplification methods. The same trained ensemble is supplied to all competing approaches. 

5.3 Simplification Methods 

RCProb is compared against established rule extraction and ensemble simplification approaches: 

• RuleCOSI+ (RC+) [13],is the deterministic rule extraction and simplification algorithm on which RCProb is built. 

• RuleFit (RFIT) [4], is a classic rule ensemble method that extracts rules from tree en-sembles and fits a sparse linear model over these rules using regularization. The Python implementation provided by the imodels package was used. 

• inTrees (IT) [5], extracts rules from tree ensembles and performs rule pruning and selec-tion based on rule quality metrics and rule interactions. The original R implementation provided by the author was used. 

• DefragTrees (DFT) [6], formulates ensemble simplification as a probabilistic model selec-tion problem, merging regions of the feature space to construct a compact interpretable model. The Python implementation provided by the authors was used. 

• A single Decision Tree (DT) as an intrinsically interpretable model baseline.

5.4 Model Evaluation and Hyperparameter Optimization 

All experiments employ a stratified 3 × 10 cross-validation scheme, corresponding to a 10-fold cross-validation repeated three times. For each fold, the tree ensemble is first trained on the training partition, and the corresponding post-hoc simplification method is then applied exclusively to the trained ensemble. The resulting interpretable model is evaluated on the held-out test partition. This protocol prevents information leakage and provides an unbiased estimate of predictive fidelity. Hyperparameters for the ensemble models and simplification methods are optimized using cross-validation within the training data. First, a 5-fold cross-validation grid search is used to tune the ensemble hyperparameters. Two parameters are considered: the number of trees 

ntrees ∈ { 10 , 25 , 50 , 100 , 250 , 500 }, and the maximum tree depth d ∈ { 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.Once the ensemble configuration is determined, an additional 3-fold cross-validation search is used to optimize the hyperparameters of the simplification and interpretable baseline methods. 5.5 Statistical Analysis 

The experimental results are validated using non-parametric statistical tests following the rec-ommendations of Demˇ sar [24]. First, an individual dataset pairwise comparison is performed us-ing the Wilcoxon signed-rank test over the 30 results obtained from the repeated cross-validation procedure. The null hypothesis assumes that, for a randomly drawn training set of fixed size, the original ensemble and the simplified model achieve equivalent classification performance on a randomly drawn test sample. Second, comparisons across multiple datasets are conducted using the Friedman test to de-termine whether statistically significant differences exist among the evaluated algorithms. When the null hypothesis is rejected, a post-hoc Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Holm’s correction is applied to control the family-wise error rate [25]. 

5.6 Performance Metrics 

Predictive performance is evaluated using standard classification metrics, including F-measure (F1) and accuracy. These metrics are widely used in the evaluation of classification models and allow direct comparison with previous rule extraction and ensemble simplification studies. To evaluate interpretability, we focus on the structural complexity of the extracted model. Following common practice in rule extraction literature, interpretability is measured using the total number of rules in the resulting ruleset. A smaller ruleset generally corresponds to a simpler and more interpretable model. For tree ensembles, the size of an ensemble can be expressed as the total number of decision rules obtained by converting each root-to-leaf path into a rule. # 6 Experimental Results 

In this section, the experimental results and their analysis are presented. The goal of the experimental evaluation is to demonstrate that the proposed probabilistic formulation preserves competitive predictive performance while improving computational practicality. 

Random Forests results Table 3 presents the predictive performance obtained when (RF) is used as the base ensemble. Among the RF-derived simplification methods, RF-RC+ achieves the highest average F1 score across the datasets. However, the difference between RF-RC+ and RF-RCProb at the aggregate level remains reasonable, indicating that the probabilistic formulation maintains competitive predictive accuracy. Furthermore, RCProb is not consistently dominated by the remaining simplification algorithms. A closer inspection of the dataset-level results shows that RF-RCProb attains the best performance on several datasets, including australian , bands , divorce , heart and ionosphere .These results suggest that the probabilistic extension preserves strong predictive behavior across diverse problems. 

Gradient boosting Machines results Table 4 reports the results obtained when GBM is used as the base ensemble. Gradient Boosting Machines, along with their variants such as XGBoost and LightGBM, are among the strongest tree-based ensemble methods in the field. Compared with the RF-based experiments, GBM-RCProb does not perform as strongly as its RF counterpart. In terms of the average F-measure across the models, it ranks fourth among the six methods considered. Nevertheless, its performance remains competitive, frequently achiev-ing results close to the best score. These results suggest that the probabilistic reformulation preserves much of the predictive strength of RC+ even when applied to a stronger ensemble backbone such as GBM. Overall, both the RF-based and GBM-based experiments indicate that the probabilistic estimation employed by RCProb does not substantially degrade predictive performance while enabling a more efficient simplification process. 

Statistical significance analysis The symbols reported in Tables 3 and 4 correspond to the Wilcoxon signed-rank test relative to the original ensemble baseline. These indicators highlight whether the observed differences are statistically significant across folds. The results show that RCProb maintains statistically comparable performance to the base-line ensemble in a large portion of the datasets. In particular, for the RF experiments 13 datasets are either statistically better or not significantly different from the baseline, while in the GBM experiments this occurs in 22 datasets. These findings further support the claim that RCProb often preserves the predictive behavior of the base ensemble while producing simplified rule-based models.

Computational efficiency. Figure 3 illustrates the computational advantage of RCProb across the 33 datasets considered in the experiments. The boxplots summarize the distribution of training or simplification times obtained for the different methods under both RF and GBM ensembles. # 7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented RCProb, a probabilistic rule extraction and simplification method designed to improve the scalability of rule-based explanations derived from tree ensembles. The proposed approach extends the RC+ framework by introducing a probabilistic formulation for rule evaluation based on Bayesian smoothing and leaf-informed estimation. This formulation replaces the repeated exact recalculation of rule statistics required during the rule combination and pruning stages with computationally efficient probabilistic estimates. As a result, the method preserves the ability to produce interpretable rule sets while significantly reducing the computational cost associated with rule simplification. An extensive experimental evaluation was conducted using multiple datasets and two widely used ensemble models, RF and GBM. The results demonstrate that RCProb maintains com-petitive predictive performance while producing highly compact rule sets. In particular, the method reduces the number of rules generated by RC+ by nearly a factor of two on average, while preserving most of the predictive performance of the original models. At the same time, RCProb provides substantial computational benefits, reducing the runtime of the rule extrac-tion and simplification process by more than one order of magnitude. These results indicate that probabilistic rule modeling provides an effective mechanism for improving the scalability of rule extraction from tree ensembles without sacrificing interpretability. Despite these encouraging results, several limitations remain. First, the current formula-tion focuses on binary classification problems, and additional work is required to extend the probabilistic rule modeling framework to multi-class settings. Second, although the evalua-tion includes a diverse set of benchmark datasets, larger-scale datasets and higher-dimensional problems could further stress the scalability of the approach. Future research should therefore investigate the behavior of the method under more demanding data conditions. The proposed framework also opens several directions for future work. One interesting extension is the incorporation of regression tasks into the probabilistic rule extraction process, which would require revisiting the mechanisms used to estimate predicted values during rule 17 Figure 3: Boxplots comparing the training or simplification time (seconds) obtained across the experiments using 33 datasets. The y-axis is shown in logarithmic scale. combination. Another promising direction is the exploration of alternative probabilistic or information-theoretic measures for rule evaluation and pruning, which could further improve the balance between predictive performance and model compactness. Finally, the probabilistic formulation introduced in RCProb may provide a foundation for incorporating uncertainty estimates into rule-based explanations, enabling future work on uncertainty-aware interpretable models for complex ensemble predictors.
